No Person, No Rights, No Responsibility: Why AI Contributors Break Our License System

Exploring a fundamental paradox in open source licensing when AI generates code

Claude Code automatically adds "Co-authored-by: Claude" to my commits. This sparked an important question: What does it mean when AI claims contributor status?

The Ownership Paradox

Here's the fundamental truth: AI cannot legally own anything.

Copyright law in both the US and EU requires human creative authorship. Since AI lacks legal personhood, it cannot hold copyright. This creates a fascinating paradox: AI-generated code may have no copyright owner‚Äîmaking it effectively public domain.

When No One Owns the Code

Traditional open source licenses assume someone holds rights to grant permissions. But when no one owns the code, the entire licensing framework breaks down:

  • MIT License: "Permission is hereby granted..." ‚Äî But who grants permission if no one owns it?
  • GPL: "You may copy and distribute..." ‚Äî Copy what? Distribute whose work?
  • Apache: "Subject to the terms..." ‚Äî Terms imposed by whom?

The Responsibility Crisis

Consider this cycle:

  1. AI generates code ‚Üí No accountability
  2. Human reviews it ‚Üí Responsibility assumed?
  3. MIT License disclaims all liability ‚Üí Accountability dissolves

Our entire licensing model assumes human authors with human rights and human responsibilities. AI breaks all three assumptions.

What Needs to Change

Current frameworks need updating for hybrid human-AI work:

  • Documentation standards: Clear attribution of AI vs. human contributions
  • Responsibility attribution: Who validates AI output?
  • New license types: Suited to the AI age
